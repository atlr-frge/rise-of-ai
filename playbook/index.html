The whole game in one move

01Why her videos look cohesive — and ours will

Her videos go viral because they look like one intentional world, not AI slop. We asked her how. Her answer, verbatim:

"That is the answer, plain and simple."
She locks one reference image — the style and color grade — and feeds it into every single generation ("match the style and grade of the attached image"). Her first approved image becomes the reference for all the others. One reference image is what makes a whole series read as a single brand. It is the proven version of the "recognizable throughout" goal we've chased from day one.

The method

04The pipeline, end to end

Script with Claude + Wispr Flow

Not a vending machine. Outline → 3 angles → draft → dictate revisions. Hooks are everything.

Film the talking head FIRST real Michael

Multiple takes, no teleprompter. Only then design the visuals.

Storyboard with Claude

Say the direction and what we don't want ("no stock B-roll"). A tactile concept → color schemes + sample prompts.

Reference the look in Flim

Search the style, download a still, give it to Claude as the grade reference.

Still first — then lock it Nano Banana Pro

Never text-to-video. Land the hero still, then make it the reference image for everything.

Animate Higgsfield · Kling 3.0 · multi-shot

"Subtle camera motion" for 90% of shots. Keep the best 1–2 seconds.

Chain with end-frames, then cut

End frame of one scene = start frame of the next (for builds). Drop into the editor. "The elegance is from the simplicity."

The tricks that matter

05Five techniques to copy exactly

Reference-image lock
One hero still in every prompt; roll the first approved image forward as the ref. The cohesion engine.
Still-first
Generate the image, get it right, then animate. Control lives in the still.
Subtle motion default
subtle camera motion + Kling 3.0 + multi-shot for ~90% of shots; add subtle subject motion for people.
End-frame chaining
For accumulating builds (a card stays, another is added), the end frame becomes the next start frame.
Tactile, not stock
Cut-outs, flat-lays, the analyst's desk. Her words: "I really don't like that typical stock-footage feel."
